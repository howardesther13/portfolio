Gun Violence

GOAL
The goal of this project is to analyze trends in gun violence across the United States, using a comprehensive dataset from 2013 to 2018, and build an interactive dashboard to visualize these trends. The analysis aims to identify high-risk areas, understand key factors contributing to gun violence, and provide actionable insights for policymakers and law enforcement agencies to reduce incidents and allocate resources effectively.
PROJECT DURATION
The project is expected to take approximately 2 months. The first month will involve data collection, cleaning, and initial exploratory analysis using Python. During the second month, advanced analytical techniques such as geospatial analysis, regression, and clustering will be applied to draw meaningful insights.
RESULT
The project will culminate in a detailed, interactive Tableau dashboard that visually showcases key findings related to gun violence trends in the United States. The dashboard will provide an in-depth look at the geographical distribution of incidents, correlations between socio-economic factors and violence, and time-based trends. In addition to the dashboard, a comprehensive report will outline insights from the analysis and offer data-driven recommendations to policymakers and law enforcement agencies on how to allocate resources more effectively. This will empower decision-makers with actionable insights aimed at reducing gun violence and improving community safety.

Insights

What trends were identified?
I analyzed gun violence data from 2013 to 2018, identifying that gun violence incidents have steadily increased over time, particularly in urban areas. California, Texas, and Illinois consistently showed high levels of gun violence, while states with lower population densities exhibited fewer incidents. A notable seasonal pattern emerged, with incidents peaking during the summer months.
How were high-risk areas identified?
Using a geospatial analysis, I mapped gun violence incidents across U.S. states and identified regional hotspots. States with higher poverty rates, larger urban populations, and higher gun ownership were found to experience higher rates of gun violence. Cluster analysis grouped states with similar characteristics, revealing that densely populated urban areas consistently rank as high-risk for gun-related incidents.

Findings

Gun Violence Trends
The analysis revealed that gun violence incidents have shown a consistent increase over the last five years, with specific hotspots emerging in urban areas. This finding can help authorities focus their efforts on high-risk regions.
Demographic Insights
Analysis indicated that gun violence disproportionately affects young males, particularly those aged 18-24. Targeted outreach and intervention programs for this demographic could be crucial in mitigating violence.
Areas for Improvement in Data Collection
Data gaps were identified, particularly in smaller, rural areas, which affected the accuracy of certain regional analyses. It's recommended to invest in more robust data collection methods to ensure comprehensive coverage across all regions.

Optimization Recommendations

Timing of Resource Allocation
To effectively reduce gun violence, resources such as law enforcement and community programs should be increased during the summer months when incidents peak. A phased approach could be implemented, with heightened resource allocation starting in May and gradually tapering off by October.
Flexible and Mobile Response Teams
Similar to emergency response units, the creation of mobile gun violence intervention teams that can be deployed to high-risk areas based on real-time data would allow for rapid responses to rising violence rates. These teams could work in partnership with local law enforcement and community organizations.
Prioritizing High-Risk Regions
By categorizing states into low-, medium-, and high-risk categories based on gun violence rates and contributing factors (poverty, urbanization, etc.), resources can be allocated more efficiently. High-risk states like California, Texas, and Illinois should receive the majority of the resources, while low-risk states can maintain current levels of intervention.
